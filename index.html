MR Hands-on

Web-based augmented reality that runs in your phone's browser. No app, no headset. You will change the code yourself and watch it break.

Before class

Do this at home. It takes about ten minutes, and if you skip it you will spend the lab watching someone else's phone.

  1. Create a GitHub account, if you do not have one.
  2. Open the repository link posted on the course page and press Fork. You now have your own copy.
  3. In your fork: Settings → Pages. Under "Build and deployment", set Source: Deploy from a branch, Branch: main, folder / (root), and press Save.
  4. Wait about a minute. Your copy is now live at
    https://<your-github-username>.github.io/<repository-name>/
  5. Open that address on your phone and check that Stage 1 asks for the camera. Bookmark it. This is the only address you need in class.

The camera only works over https://. Opening the HTML file directly from your laptop's disk will never work — that is not a bug in the code, it is the browser refusing to hand a local file your camera.

Markers

Two markers, A and B. Print them or show them on a screen — marker sheet. Paper works better than a screen: a screen adds glare and flicker that the tracker has to fight.

The stages

Stage 1 · Primitives Put a box on Marker A. Change its colour, size, position, spin. Stage 2 · 3D model Swap the box for an animated 3D fox. Change which animation plays. Stage 3 · Two markers Track A and B at once. Make them react when they get close.

How to change the code

  1. On your laptop, open your fork on GitHub and click the file you want (for example stage1.html).
  2. Click the pencil icon, edit, then Commit changes.
  3. Wait roughly 30–60 seconds — GitHub has to republish the page — then reload on your phone.

That wait is real, so batch your edits: change three things, then commit once. While you wait, do the observations below.

Observe how it breaks

All of these are supposed to fail. Failing is the experiment. For each one, note what happened and why, and connect it to the problems from the first lecture.

  1. Tilt. Slowly tilt the marker away from the camera. At roughly what angle does tracking die? Why does a flat pattern become hard to recognise when you look at it edge-on?
  2. Occlusion of the marker. Cover half the marker with your hand. Does it survive? Now cover 90%. Where is the limit?
  3. Light. Dim the room. Then put a bright light or window directly behind the marker. Which one hurts more, and why?
  4. Occlusion of the object. Put your hand between the phone and the virtual object. Does your hand pass in front of it, or does the object float on top of your hand? Which of the MR problems from lecture 1 is this?
  5. Lighting consistency. Does the virtual object look like it is lit by the same light as the room around it? What would you need to know about the room to make it look right?
  6. Motion. Shake the phone, or move it fast. Does the object stay glued to the marker, or lag behind and swim?
  7. Units (stage 3). The readout says "marker widths". A distance of 1.0 means the two markers are exactly touching. Why is that a more useful unit here than centimetres?

If it does not work

  • No camera prompt. Check the address really starts with https://. On iPhone, Safari asks once per site; if you said no, go to Settings → Safari → Camera and reset it, then reload.
  • Black screen. Another tab or app probably still holds the camera. Close the others and reload.
  • Camera is on but nothing appears. Fill more of the frame with the marker, hold it flatter, and give it better light.
  • Your edit does nothing. Did the republish finish? Check the Actions tab of your fork. Also try a hard reload.
  • You broke it beyond repair. On GitHub, open the file's History and revert to an earlier version. Nothing here is precious.
